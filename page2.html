My Automata Project

What is Automata?
Automata is the plural of the word automaton, which is defined as a piece of machinery that can operate on its own with little to no human input required.
My Automata Project
The purpose of this specific automata project was to create a simplistic game out of cardboard that can be played by anyone with access to a battery.

-Day 1-

Day 1
Day 1 started with a basic presentation of the specific game I wanted to make though a software prototype. The workload started with putting together the basic backdrop for the game. I had to skin and cut some wires that were necessary for the collision system to work. Using aluminum tape, I pasted the wires onto the paper, in such a way that they matched the visual representation shown on the other side of the page.

—Day 2—

Day 2
Day 2 started with attaching a dynamo to a paper towel roll. This was done in order to put together the basic rotation system for the game to function. I attached rubber bands to each of the rolls, in order to maximize the friction between the paper and the roll, in order to get a smooth rotation.
I cut some cardboards to act as supports for the 2 rolls, and attached them to the sides of a larger piece of cardboard I had obtained prior to starting the project.
Next up was creating the begginings of what would become the wheel system. I hot glued a bearing onto the piece of cardboard, got another paper towel roll, and attached it to the sides of the bearing in order to get a working wheel.
Finally, I had to attach the paper onto the rolls and the rolls onto the supports in order to get the full skeleton working.

—Day 3—

Day 3
Day 3 started with me having to cut out a metal rod to use for the project. This was mean't to be manipulated by the wheel, and act as a way for the player to control their avatar.
I cut out a few pieces of cardboard to use as support structures later, and attached them to the frame. I then skinned more wires, and kept the skin. I then slid the wire skin onto the edges of the rod, and attached them to the support structures, such that the rod stays anchored to the play area.
I then wrapped a piece of string around my wheel, and attached it to the rod, in such a way that the wheel would be able to move the rod around the play field. Afterwards, I attached another piece of string on the rod, and above the background, to attach the player to later.
I finally attached a piece of aluminum onto the bottom of the rod, in order to act as the player's hitbox.

—Day 4—

Day 4
Day 4 started with me creating the player visual, and attaching it onto the string, such that it lined up with it's hitbox. I then had to solder a power source to the dynamo, such that it would be able to rotate the background and allow the game to start. Finally, I adjusted a few elements, and attached a battery, and the project was functional and finished.

Problems That Came Up During the Project

I encountered many different difficulties as the project went on.

Day 1

During day 1, we had an issue where the paper base I had printed was too small to be used in the larger planned project, requiring a noticable decrease in scale when compared to the original plan.


Day 2

Day 2 involved the creation of the rotation system used to make the paper background appear to be scrolling. I encountered many issues with the exact placement of the rollers, as they had to be pretty precise in order to get the desired smooth rotation. This was resolved by pure trial and error, which resulted in me having to take the entire system apart and put it back together multiple times.


Day 3

Day 3 suffered pretty badly from the downscaling done previously, as that resulted in issues involving the system originally intended for the wheel becoming literally impossible to pull off. This was resolved by using a wire to extend the cardboard supports. Also worth noting was that I had no access to the aluminum coke can intended in the original plan, resulting in me having to improvise by wrapping aluminum tape around a newspaper.


Day 4

Day 4 had probably the most issues. For one, the dynamo we installed back in day 2 was too weak to properly rotate the whole system, resulting in me having to create an entirely new frankenstein's monster of a dynamo by combining a stronger one with the frame of the original. Even still, the load was too much, resulting in me having to essentially redo practically everything from day 2, in order to lessen the load. Finally, came the fact that the soldering iron I bought was defective, and heated up at the wrong areas, resulting in me having to obtain another one. 

Throughout the project, I learned quite a few neat bits of information. For one, I learned that I am more well suited to software design than I am on hardware. Seriously though, the most important lesson I learned from this project was how to act when the plan falls apart.

During the creation of any project, any plans that I had made will eventually fall, and this project was no exception. Wether its the size discrepancy, weak or straight up broken equipment, or even having to redo the whole thing from scratch, this project had many moments where my plans fell apart. What this project taught me was how to adapt during these situations. Instead of giving up, or restarting, instead find a way to make it work. The improvisational mindset that I had to shift to during moments of chaos sometimes even resulted in me creating something that ended up better than the original idea.

By the end, I was able to fully finish an actual working product, which is more than can be said for some other failed projects of mine. Overall, I'd say this project helped propel my designer mindset's evolution greatly.
