Pretty Much My Life Story

Prologue
My Begginings

My journey towards Game design starts at around 2011. I was seven years old at the time, and was looking for something to do. It was then that I found a piece of software entitled "GameMaker 8.1". I was already avidly playing and enjoying games at this point, so the prospect of being able to make one of my own was appealing to me. I installed it, and looked up a few tutorials online on how the program worked. For most of my early projects, I made use of the engine's drag and drop functionality that allowed for games to be made with no code. I would later learn to use the engine's programming language in order to optimize the game better.

Part 1
The Maze Games
(2010)

 I was able to learn the ropes quickly, and threw together a pretty rudimentary maze game. It had quite a few flaws, including:

  • Stiff Controls
  • Innacurate collision detection
  • Unappealing Art Direction

None of these flaws really mattered at the time. What mattered was that I was able to make something that worked. I continued to make a few more of these simple maze games, each getting marginally better.Despite the increase in quality, I was starting to drift away from simple maze games, and instead started moving towards larger projects.

Part 2
Fan Game Frenzy
(2011—2012)

The entire reason I started getting into game development in the first place, was so that I could make fan games for some of my favorite franchises at the time. It is because of this I enthusiastically started to work on a few fan projects, shortly after getting accustomed to the engine.

For those Unaware a Fan game is a game created by a dedicated fan who simply wants to create something based in the universe of their favorite franchise.

—Blitz Sonic Adventure—

The first of these fan projects was a sonic fangame entitled Blitz Sonic Adventure. I was able to create a few buggy levels, and a single boss fight for this one before I stopped development. This was the only one of these fangames not made entirely by me, as I used a publicly available project file that handled the basic gameplay for me.

—Untitled Megaman Fangame—

The next fangame I made was an unamed megaman fangame. Unlike the previous game, everything aside from the game assets was done by me (for better or for worse). The game is mostly contained within a single small room, where the player can move around and shoot. I was able to program the basics of a platformer, plus the basic shooting mechanics. However the game suffers from iffy collision detection and sluggish controls.

—Untitled FNAF Fangame—

The last of these fangames was a working FNAF fangame. This one is probably the most impressive of my fangames, as just about everything from this game was made by me —from the assets to the code. I was able to implement a single animatronic, and program it's movements between areas, as well as the basic camera and door mechanics. 

Developing some of these fangames exposed me to many of the intracacies that go into the games that I admired and how precise a lot of elements had to be in order for them to even function properly.
Part 3
Attack of the E0 Virus
(2013—2016)

After a bunch of failed fangames, my nine year old self wanted to start making a larger scale project. That Project would come to be known as, Attack of the E0 Virus.

—How I Started—

I started the project with some basic concepts and designs relating to the story and world of the game. In terms of gameplay, E0 was mean't to be a 2D sidescrolling shooter, in the vain of games like gradius, but with bullet hell elements from games like tohou. The story was mean't to chronicle the rise of an intelligent virus by the name of E0, and their eventual downfall by the hands of the game's team of main characters. The game was planned to have a total of nine levels, each with a unique boss at the end. There were even mean't to be multiple unlockable ships in the game.

A friend of mine helped with designing the appearance of certain enemies and locations, while another helped by play testing the game. The game was of a large scale, and in was in hindsight, too much for me to handle, but I thought at the time that I could do it.

I started work by designing the main ship of the game. It started off in a pixel art style, and was made mostly using the default shape tools, as I had little to no skill in art at the time. Despite the amateurish nature of this design, It's probably the design that has stuck with me the most, long after the project had come and gone.

In terms of programming, I was able to make a basic system for sidescrolling shmups, which allowed for expansion to be relatively simple. Enemies, bosses and player characters made use of the Engine's parenting feature which mean't that global changes to the game were relatively easy to make. This game also marked the first time I started using the engine's programming language GML, as it allowed for better control of game assets, and resulted in smaller file sizes, and higher framerates.

I had actually thrown together a basic soundtrack for the game, using Garageband Mobile, as it was all I had at the time. Unfortunately, this soundtrack remains lost, as my phone died.

—What Went Well—

Throughout the course of E0's development, I picked up on a bunch of new skills. Most of them revolved around asset creation and management. One of the most important events that occured during this game's development was my introduction to Blender. This program allowed me to make 3D recreations of all of the game's assets at the time. It is because of this introduction that the game later shifted to having a pre-rendered 3D look, though it still looked quite rough as I was still quite inexperienced with the program.

—Development Hell—

The game was initially mean't to only take around 6 months, however development continued on to around 3 years. It was at this point that I had practically given up on the project. I was unsatisfied with the game's quality and level design, yet I was too afraid to undo years of work.


In the end, I decided to pull the plug on development. The announcement had little fanfare, as no one really knew of the work I put into the game, and my friends who were once estatic to help in the project ended up jumping ship mid way early in development.


It was then that I decided to take a break from game development for a while. This game burned be out to the point that I didn't come back until about a year later.

Part 4
The Return
(2017)
in 2017, around a year after the cancellation of E0, I decided to start jumping back into game development, since I started getting interested in the design aspect of games at this point.

—Gamemaker Studio 1.4—

I started my journey back into game development, with the engine I started with. Gamemaker, now called Gamemaker Studio had changed considerably from the time I first installed it.

With this engine, I made a few prototypes with some basic gameplay. I gave special attention to make sure the games' controls were at an acceptable level, and not the stiff abominations from my past.

—Unity 3D—

It is at this point that I started experimenting with other engines, starting with the ever popular Unity Engine.

This was where I first started experimenting with making 3D games. I made a few first person prototypes, and 2 platformers, one with a free camera, and another with a fixed camera. I also payed special attention to the games' visuals, and played around with the post processing settings, and occasionally making use of some free assets from the asset store, while still making sure to keep the game running at a constant framerate.

Part 5
Present Day
(2018—Present)
In 2018, 2 years after the cancellation of E0, I decided to start work on a new major project. I decided to start small, with a simple arcade type game, entitled Terminal Defense.

—Terminal Defense—

This game has players running around the area collecting energy orbs to stay alive, and killing enemies with their own projectiles. The player's health is constantly depleting, incentivizing them to collect health through energy orbs and killing enemies. The game requires players to constantly be on the move, and to dispose of enemies quickly.

My goal with this game was to provide a simple, but thrilling fast paced experience for the player, and I personally believe I succeeded in this goal.

I started joining game jams shortly after, and most of my newest projects are made for certain Jams.

—5 Minutes to Escape the Hole—

5 Minutes to Escape the Hole was developed for the Dallas Dream Hack Game Jam. The game consists of players running around disposing of enemies with projectile attacks. Players are required to outlast the wave of foes for 5 minutes before the game ends. at around 3 minutes, the player is required to go up against the final boss which puts to test the skills the player has gained throughout the game. All the assets for the game are pre-rendered 3D models, made and animated in blender.

—The Exploration of the Badly Drawn Ruins—

The Exploration of the Badly Drawn Ruins was developed for the I Can't Draw Game Jam, which explains the rough art style. The game consists of players exploring the ruins, looking for keys to unlock doors, while going through elaborately designed obstacle courses featuring a plethora of distinct enemies, each with different behaviors. When designing this game, I payed special attention to User Experience. 

I balanced the game in such a way that the player is eased into the experience, before being shoved into the challenging labarynth. I even playtested this game with young children to test the User Experience, as they tend to be easily frustrated by difficult games. They did have trouble, but they persevered through the game, and reported enjoying the overall experience.

—The Epic Adventure of a Dapp Boi and his Goth GF—

The Epic Adventure of a Dapp Boi and his Goth GF is an RPG Maker game I made mostly as a joke for my friends. Despite it's Joke Game status, I decided to use this game as an opportunity to practice game balancing. The game starts as extremely simple, and ends with a pretty difficult final boss.

—Untitled Top Down Shooter—

Untitled Top Down Shooter is my latest completed project, despite it's Untitled state. This game retains the frantic design philosophy from Terminal Defense, but applying it to a different genre. When making this game, I payed special attention to Game Feel. As such, most of the visual elements will have a noticable response to certain actions. Your health value will bounce around when being damaged, the screen will shake violently when you release a large attack, and enemy deaths result in a flurry of neon colored explosions. 

This is probably my favorite of my games visually, despite it's simple flat colored aesthetic.

"Looking back through my past projects has shown me that I have evolved considerably since the days of simple maze games. My hope is to continue to evolve past the point I'm at now, such that I can execute on my own ideas, and tell the world the narratives I've conceptualized, in my art form of choice"
